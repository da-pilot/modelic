// 03 / The brief
What SiriusXM came to us with.
"Our subscription flow was working well at scale, and that was the problem. Improvements that look small in percentage points are large at our volume. We needed a partner who could run experiments with us, not for us."
// 04 / Discovery
What we learned.
The existing flow had been optimized over years — the easy wins were already taken. The remaining wins were architectural: places where the flow assumed a desktop-first user, places where the trial-conversion language was technically clear but emotionally cold, places where the funnel collapsed on the smallest screens.
We ran the audit alongside the SiriusXM analytics team — their data, our pattern recognition. The recommendations that came out of the engagement were paired: each one had a measurable hypothesis attached, and a kill criterion if the hypothesis didn't pan out.
// 05 / Build
A/B testing as a partnership, not a product.
We didn't bring an A/B testing platform. SiriusXM had one. We brought the discipline of writing tests with kill criteria up front — what we expected to see, what would falsify it, what the next experiment would be in either case.
The work shipped as a series of small, measurable changes — not as a redesign. Each shipped variant earned its place in the flow by moving a number, or got rolled back. The cumulative effect, over six months, was a meaningful subscription-flow improvement at national scale.
// 06 / What we decided
Two load-bearing calls.
We resisted a redesign. The temptation, on a flow this old, is to rebuild from scratch. We argued for incremental — and the SiriusXM team backed it. The reason: a rebuild would have lost the historical baseline data, and without that we'd be testing in the dark for six months.
We made the trial-conversion copy warmer. The original copy was technically accurate and emotionally absent. We rewrote — and tested — language that acknowledged the customer was about to be charged. The empathy was measurable.
// 07 / What changed
