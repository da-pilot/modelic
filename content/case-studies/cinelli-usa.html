// 03 / The brief
What Cinelli USA came to us with.
"Cinelli is a 75-year-old Italian brand. The US edition needed to honor that — without becoming a museum. We needed a partner who understood the difference."
// 04 / Discovery
What we learned.
Cinelli's heritage is real. Their relationship with the cycling community is real. What was missing was a US storefront that connected the global brand language to a domestic distribution and shipping reality. The previous site read as a translated Italian site — accurate, but not at home.
We also found the audience was bifurcated: high-end track and road cyclists who wanted the full Cinelli archive, and city-bike commuters who wanted a single beautiful frame. Both were buying. The site was treating them as the same customer.
// 05 / Brand
Heritage without nostalgia.
The visual system leans on the Cinelli archive — typography from the 1960s catalogs, race photography from the 1970s — but treats them as living material, not museum pieces. The storefront feels like a contemporary city-bike shop that happens to know its history.
Two product paths run in parallel. The "Archive" thread serves the collector and competitive customer. The "City" thread serves the commuter. Both share the same visual language — the difference is in the depth of detail and the photography.
// 06 / What we decided
Two load-bearing calls.
We didn't make a heritage section. The temptation, with a 75-year archive, is to wall it off into a "heritage" page. We argued for integration — the heritage shows up across the site, not in a separate room.
We split the customer paths without splitting the brand. Two product threads, one identity. The customer chooses their path; the brand stays consistent on either.
